What is Treaty 3?

Treaty 3, also known as the Northwest Angle Treaty, was signed in 1873 between the Canadian Crown and the Ojibwe (Anishinaabe) peoples of the Lake of the Woods and Rainy River regions in what is now northwestern Ontario and a small part of Manitoba. It was one of the Numbered Treaties negotiated following Confederation, as Canada sought to open the land for settlement and the construction of the Canadian Pacific Railway. Under the treaty, the Ojibwe ceded approximately 55,000 square miles of territory in exchange for promises of reserve lands, annual payments, farming equipment, schools, and the right to continue hunting and fishing on ceded lands. Treaty 3 is particularly notable because the Ojibwe negotiators, led by Chief Powasson and others, pushed back firmly against the Crown's initial terms and secured more favourable conditions than earlier treaties had offered — making it an important moment in Indigenous treaty negotiation history. Today, the treaty area is governed under the framework of the Grand Council Treaty #3, which continues to advocate for the rights and interests of its member First Nations.

Why Do People Need to Know About Treaty 3?

People need to know about Treaty 3 because it is an important part of the history and relationship between Indigenous peoples and the government of Canada. The treaty explains how land in parts of northwestern Ontario and southeastern Manitoba was shared between the Canadian government and the Anishinaabe Nation. Understanding Treaty 3 helps people learn about the promises that were made to First Nations, such as land rights, annual payments, and the ability to hunt and fish on traditional lands. It is also important today because these treaties still affect laws, land use, and Indigenous rights in Canada. Learning about it helps people better understand Canadian history and the responsibilities that come from these agreements.
